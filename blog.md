**What do you work on?**

My research focuses on how the maternal immune system changes during pregnancy, especially how T cells adapt in different tissues such as the intestine, spleen, and placenta. I am particularly interested in how immune cells help maintain a healthy balance: protecting the mother and fetus from infection while also supporting fetal tolerance and proper pregnancy outcomes.

**Why did you choose to work in this field?**

Pregnancy is one of the most fascinating immunological states. The maternal immune system must tolerate the fetus, which carries paternal antigens, but at the same time it still needs to defend both the mother and fetus against pathogens. There are still many unknowns about how immune cells behave at the maternal-fetal interface, which makes it an exciting area for discovery.

**What would you say to a college freshman thinking about going into this field?**

I would tell them that maternal-fetal immunology is a very exciting and meaningful field with many unanswered questions. It connects immunology, development, microbiology, reproductive biology, and human health. If you are curious about how the immune system can be both protective and tolerant at the same time, this is a great field to explore.

**What do you work on?**

I study neurodevelopment, focusing on how maternal factors — particularly immune cells and cytokines — shape brain development and produce lasting effects on offspring behavior and neural function across the lifespan.

**Why did you choose to work in this field?**

I have always been fascinated by the nervous system and by how it evolved and interacts with other systems, like the immune and endocrine systems. Neuroimmunology at the maternal-fetal interface brings these interests together: it offers the chance to understand how maternal biology influences fetal brain development and how pregnancy influences maternal health. That complexity and translational potential are what drew me to this area.

**What would you say to a college freshman thinking about going into this field?**

It's an exciting but still underexplored field with many opportunities. There's a real need for new tools and mechanistic studies to distinguish homeostatic processes from pathological ones in pregnancy. Working here means contributing to research that could directly improve maternal health, protect children's neurodevelopment, and identify biomarkers or interventions to prevent disease. If you enjoy interdisciplinary science — developmental biology, immunology, neuroscience, and clinical translation — this is a great place to be.
