Voices from the maternal–fetal interface researchers.

Conversations with researchers and clinicians working at the frontiers of the field, and reflections from the team building this site.

Ai-Ris Yonekura Collier, M.D.

OB-GYN & MFM Specialist · Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center · Harvard Medical School

Studying immune dysregulation in preeclampsia and transplacental antibody transfer

What do you work on?

I'm a physician-scientist studying how the human immune system adapts during pregnancy. My work focuses on making sure pregnant people are included in research (so we can improve care and outcomes for both the parent and baby.

Why did you choose this field?

I fell in love with science in high school and became fascinated by immunology in college. I'm also a creative, hands-on person, so I was drawn to work that blends thinking and doing. In medical school, I discovered obstetrics (and it clicked. Pregnancy, like immunology, is all about adaptation. Now I get to combine both: as a researcher, I study how the immune system supports a developing fetus, and as a clinician, I care for patients from pregnancy through postpartum using skills like ultrasound, procedures, and surgery.

What would you say to a freshman interested in immunology but new to pregnancy immunology?

Pregnancy is one of the most fascinating immunology problems in nature. The body carries a genetically distinct fetus without rejecting it (and even transfers protective antibodies to support the baby. If you're curious about how the immune system balances tolerance and protection, pregnancy is an incredible place to explore.

Douglas Kwon, M.D., Ph.D.

Associate Professor · Ragon Institute of MGH, MIT, and Harvard · Harvard Medical School

Studying mucosal immunology, the microbiome, and women's reproductive health

What do you work on?

I am an infectious disease trained clinician-scientist studying how the immune system interacts with the microbiome at mucosal surfaces, particularly in the female reproductive tract and gut. My lab focuses on understanding how these interactions shape inflammation, susceptibility to infection, pregnancy outcomes, and responses to therapies and vaccines. Much of our work aims to translate basic immunology and microbiome science into new approaches to improve women's health globally, especially in low- and middle-income regions where infectious and reproductive health burdens are often highest.

Why did you choose to work in this field?

I was initially drawn to immunology because the immune system sits at the intersection of so many areas of biology and medicine. Over time, I became increasingly interested in mucosal immunology because these tissues are where the body constantly negotiates interactions with microbes, the environment, and other people. Women's health research felt both deeply important clinically and an area where there were significant opportunities to translate basic scientific understanding into meaningful improvements in health outcomes. There are still many major unanswered questions in this field, and historically women's health has been underfunded and understudied despite its enormous global impact. I've also been fortunate to work closely with outstanding collaborators and clinical researchers in this space, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, which reinforced how much meaningful translational work remains to be done.

What would you say to a college freshman thinking about going into this field?

Women's health and maternal-fetal immunology are incredibly exciting fields because they combine fundamental biology with direct clinical relevance and global impact. Pregnancy, reproduction, and host–microbe interactions raise some of the most fascinating questions in immunology. My advice would be to seek out mentors working in areas that deeply resonate with you and not worry too much about having a perfectly defined path from the beginning. Some of the most interesting science happens at the intersection of disciplines, so building broad skills and perspectives can be very valuable. It is also a field where there is still tremendous opportunity to make discoveries that can meaningfully improve people's lives.

Shanshan Zhang, Ph.D.

Postdoctoral Researcher · Huh Lab · Harvard Medical School

Studying interactions between maternal and fetal immune cells

What do you work on?

My research focuses on how the maternal immune system changes during pregnancy, especially how T cells adapt in different tissues such as the intestine, spleen, and placenta. I am particularly interested in how immune cells help maintain a healthy balance: protecting the mother and fetus from infection while also supporting fetal tolerance and proper pregnancy outcomes.

Why did you choose to work in this field?

Pregnancy is one of the most fascinating immunological states. The maternal immune system must tolerate the fetus, which carries paternal antigens, but at the same time it still needs to defend both the mother and fetus against pathogens. There are still many unknowns about how immune cells behave at the maternal-fetal interface, which makes it an exciting area for discovery.

What would you say to a college freshman who is thinking about going into the field of immunology at the maternal-fetal interface?

I would tell them that maternal-fetal immunology is a very exciting and meaningful field with many unanswered questions. It connects immunology, development, microbiology, reproductive biology, and human health. If you are curious about how the immune system can be both protective and tolerant at the same time, this is a great field to explore.

Ines Zalosnik, Ph.D.

Postdoctoral Researcher · Huh Lab · Harvard Medical School

Studying maternal immune activation and fetal neurodevelopment

What do you work on?

I study neurodevelopment, focusing on how maternal factors, particularly immune cells and cytokines, shape brain development and produce lasting effects on offspring behavior and neural function across the lifespan.

Why did you choose to work in this field?

I have always been fascinated by the nervous system, from invertebrates to humans, and by how it evolved and interacts with other systems, like the immune and endocrine systems. Neuroimmunology at the maternal-fetal interface brings these interests together: it offers the chance to understand how maternal biology influences fetal brain development and how pregnancy influences maternal health. That complexity and translational potential are what drew me to this area.

What would you say to a college freshman who is thinking about going into the field of immunology at the maternal-fetal interface?

It's an exciting but still underexplored field with many opportunities. There's a real need for new tools and mechanistic studies to distinguish homeostatic processes from pathological ones in pregnancy. Working here means contributing to research that could directly improve maternal health, protect children's neurodevelopment, and identify biomarkers or interventions to prevent disease. If you enjoy interdisciplinary science, developmental biology, immunology, neuroscience, and clinical translation, this is a great place to be.

By Sarah Syed · April 2026

From the Lab to the Clinic: Reflections on My First ACOG Annual Meeting

Last weekend, I had the privilege of attending the 75th Annual American College of Obstetrics and Gynecology (ACOG) Annual Clinical and Scientific Meeting for the first time. The conference was held in Washington, DC, and for someone like me, a PhD student steeped in immunology papers and mouse models, stepping into a world of 6,000 clinicians was both humbling and incredibly exciting.

What Is ACOG, and Why Does It Matter?

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists is the leading professional organization for OB-GYNs in the United States, representing over 60,000 physicians and women's health care professionals. Founded in 1951, ACOG's mission is to advance women's health through continuous professional education, rigorous clinical practice standards, and advocacy for health equity. Its annual meeting, the ACSM, is the flagship event where clinicians gather to share the latest in clinical practice, emerging science, and the very real challenges of caring for women across the lifespan.

I went in expecting to observe and learn. I left inspired to continue working in maternal-fetal immunology.

Caring for Women Across a Lifetime of Gaps

Sitting through session after session, one theme became impossible to ignore: the gaps in care for women are not small, and they are not new. Adolescence, pregnancy, the postpartum period, menopause—at every stage, there are conditions that remain poorly understood, poorly diagnosed, and poorly treated.

Endometriosis. Polycystic ovary syndrome. Preeclampsia. These are not rare diseases. They affect millions of women, often for years or decades. And yet, the honest truth that panelists returned to again and again was this: we don't really know what causes them. For endometriosis and PCOS, the mainstay of treatment in 2025 remains hormonal birth control and NSAIDs, interventions that manage symptoms but do nothing to address the underlying pathology. Multiple speakers, across multiple sessions, paused to acknowledge this with a kind of practiced candor: the field is treating downstream effects because it does not yet understand the root cause.

Preeclampsia, one of the most dangerous complications of pregnancy and a leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, was often framed not as a problem with a cure on the horizon, but as a condition to be anticipated and managed. The most optimistic near-term interventions on the table were early diagnosis and risk stratification, knowing sooner who will get sick, rather than knowing how to prevent it. That is progress, and it matters. But it also tells you something about where the field stands scientifically.

What I Saw on the Exhibit Floor

The conference was predominantly clinical in its orientation, which reflects both ACOG's core mission and the current state of women's health innovation. I didn't see a large therapeutic presence (yet), but what I did see was a meaningful and growing diagnostics space: a diagnostic for preeclampsia from Thermo Fisher Scientific that caught considerable attention, and two separate companies focused specifically on endometriosis diagnostics. The fact that companies are investing in identifying these diseases more precisely tells you something: we know the biology is there, waiting to be decoded. These diagnostics tell me that we are at the beginning of real change in this space.

The Feeling in the Room

But if I had to describe ACOG in a single word, it would be passionate.

You could feel it everywhere, in the standing-room-only sessions (every session was so packed that there were multiple overflow rooms), in the hallway conversations that spilled past the scheduled breaks, in the faces of physicians who have spent decades caring for women with the tools they have, wishing for tools they don't. There is a deep love for this work in this community. There is also a clear-eyed, unsentimental understanding that the field has a very long way to go.

That combination, passionate dedication paired with honest acknowledgment of how much remains unknown, is exactly what makes a field ready to change.

What Is Next for OB-GYN?

Throughout the conference, I made a point of talking to OB-GYNs about my own research: maternal-fetal immunology, and specifically the role of the immune system in pregnancy complications. The immune interface between mother and fetus is one of the most extraordinary and least understood phenomena in human biology. Pregnancy requires the body to tolerate a genetically foreign organism, to remodel an organ from scratch, to calibrate inflammation with extraordinary precision, and when something goes wrong at that interface, the consequences can be preeclampsia, preterm birth, recurrent pregnancy loss, or fetal growth restriction.

Every time I described this work to a clinician, I heard some version of the same sentence: "I'm so glad someone is finally looking into this. This is what the field needs."

That response, repeated across dozens of conversations, told me something important. The clinicians treating these conditions every day have an intuition, built from years of patient care, that the answers lie somewhere in the biology of the maternal immune system. They just haven't had scientists working alongside them to find them.

The Field This Conference Deserves

My hope, the hope that drove me to come to ACOG in the first place, and that I'll carry back into the lab, is to be part of transforming OB-GYN from a specialty that is primarily reactive to one that is mechanistically understood. Right now, much of what OB-GYNs can offer are downstream interventions: manage the symptoms, monitor the pregnancy, respond to the crisis. That is not a failure of the clinicians. It is a consequence of how little basic science has been done in this space, relative to other fields.

The future I want to see is one where new immunological insights translate into new therapies, where diseases like endometriosis are understood at a cellular and molecular level, where preeclampsia has not just an early diagnostic but an early intervention, where the gap between what we observe in the clinic and what we understand in the lab is finally closed.

I hope to come back to ACOG someday and see that future on the exhibit floor, not just better diagnostics, but real therapeutics born from real science. Not just clinicians managing outcomes, but scientists and clinicians working together to change them.

That day isn't here yet. But the passion to get there certainly is.
