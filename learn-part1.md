## What is the immune system?

<div class="video-wrap">
<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/PzunOgYHeyg" title="What is the immune system?" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
</div>

Your body is constantly under siege. Every time you breathe, eat, or touch a surface, you're exposed to bacteria, viruses, fungi, and other microorganisms that would happily use your cells as a home and food source. The immune system is your body's defense network — a remarkable collection of cells, proteins, and organs whose job is to tell the difference between you and not you, and to destroy anything that doesn't belong. This idea, called **self vs. non-self recognition**, is the single most important concept in immunology. Every cell in your body carries a molecular ID tag on its surface (made of proteins called MHC molecules) that essentially says "I belong here." Immune cells constantly scan for these tags — anything without the right tag is treated as a potential threat.

The immune system actually has two distinct layers that work together. The first is the **innate immune system** — your fast, non-specific first responder. Within minutes of detecting something foreign, innate immune cells like neutrophils and macrophages rush to the site and begin attacking, releasing chemicals that cause the redness and swelling we call inflammation. The second layer is the **adaptive immune system**, which is slower but far more precise. Adaptive immune cells — T cells and B cells — learn to recognize the specific shape of a particular invader, mount a targeted attack, and then remember that invader. This is why a vaccine works: you never get the real infection, but your adaptive immune system still learns to recognize it.

These two systems talk to each other constantly. Together, they make up a system of remarkable sophistication. And here's the puzzle we'll spend the rest of this course on: all of this elegant self-vs-non-self machinery has to be somehow suspended — or at least renegotiated — during pregnancy. The fetus is, genetically, half its father. By the rules we just described, it should be recognized as foreign. It isn't. Why not?

## Recognizing the enemy

<div class="video-wrap">
<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/rIrjO3q4Y9c" title="How the immune system recognizes threats" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
</div>

Imagine the immune system as a highly trained security force that has to distinguish between millions of possible intruders. It does this using shape. Every molecule in your body has a three-dimensional structure, and the immune system is exquisitely sensitive to shapes it doesn't recognize. These foreign molecular shapes are called **antigens**. Specialized cells called **dendritic cells** and macrophages are constantly patrolling your tissues, and when they encounter something suspicious, they engulf it, break it into fragments, and display those fragments on their surface like wanted posters. This act, called **antigen presentation**, is the critical handoff from the innate system to the adaptive one.

Those "wanted posters" are recognized by T cells. Each T cell carries a unique receptor on its surface, shaped to fit one specific antigen fragment. Your body produces millions of T cells with different receptors, collectively capable of recognizing almost any antigen — a strategy called **clonal diversity**. **Killer T cells** hunt down and destroy any body cell displaying that antigen. **Helper T cells** act as commanders, releasing chemical signals called cytokines that coordinate and amplify the entire immune response.

**B cells** are the immune system's antibody factories. When a B cell is activated it matures into a plasma cell and starts churning out antibodies — Y-shaped proteins released into the bloodstream by the millions. Antibodies work by binding to antigens and physically blocking them (neutralization), coating pathogens so macrophages can eat them (opsonization), or triggering a cascade of destructive proteins called complement. Each antibody fits only one antigen, making the immune response remarkably precise. And when the infection clears, memory B and T cells remain, ready to mount a faster response if that antigen ever appears again.

## Learning not to attack yourself

<div class="video-wrap">
<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/po9t3F5g-vk?start=40" title="Immune tolerance" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
</div>

<div class="video-wrap">
<iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/2jEBTeAgtFY?start=173" title="Regulatory T cells" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
</div>

The immune system faces a fundamental design problem: it needs to be aggressive enough to destroy pathogens, but restrained enough not to destroy the body it's protecting. The solution is **tolerance**: a set of mechanisms that teach immune cells to ignore "self" antigens. The first layer happens in the thymus, where T cells are born and educated through a process called **central tolerance**. Immature T cells are exposed to samples of the body's own proteins — if a T cell reacts too strongly, it gets deleted. This **clonal deletion** eliminates roughly 95% of all T cells produced. Only cells that are functional but non-reactive to self survive.

But central tolerance isn't perfect. Some self-reactive T cells slip through. This is where **peripheral tolerance** takes over, operating in the tissues and bloodstream throughout life. One key mechanism is **anergy**: if a T cell encounters its antigen without the right co-stimulatory signals, it becomes unresponsive rather than activated. Another is suppression by **regulatory T cells, or Tregs** — the immune system's peacekeepers. Tregs actively patrol and dampen immune responses nearby, releasing anti-inflammatory signals and suppressing other T cells.

Tregs are going to be central characters for the rest of this course. They are the mechanism by which the immune system tolerates things it has decided not to attack. They show up wherever the immune system needs to hold back — chronic infections, tumors trying to evade immunity, and, critically, pregnancy. The uterus during pregnancy is packed with Tregs. They accumulate there specifically in response to paternal antigens on fetal cells, and experimental studies in mice show that depleting Tregs causes pregnancy loss.
